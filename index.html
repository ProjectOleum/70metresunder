The title of this stuff

One of Canada’s most valuable economic resources was deemed worthless at first glance.
   In 1719, a Cree man named Wapasu brought a sample of bitumen to Henry Kelsey, a Hudson Bay Company explorer. Bitumen -- a dark, tarlike substance composed of sand, clay, oil and water -- would naturally seep out of the Athabasca River banks, its presence long known by Aboriginal Peoples in Canada. Wapasu’s sample, which was likely the first bitumen encountered by Europeans, was considered useless.
  Three centuries later, bitumen has proven its worth on a global scale. The oil sands play a dominant part in Canada’s energy sector and the oil harvested from the sands comprises 97 per cent of Canada’s proven oil reserves. Oil is in constant global demand, and Canada has heavily invested and exploited its oil sands to meet that growing need.

  Bitumen is economically valuable, in high demand and available for extraction. It’s proponents call it a natural resource meant to be utilized for the good of the Canadian economy; it’s critics call it an environmental nightmare and an exploitation of Canada’s natural resources.
  Prime Minister Justin Trudeau stoked the flames of debate earlier this year when he publicly called for the oil sands to be “phased out.” He later retracted the statement, but the anger and the questions sparked by his remark illustrate depth of support and concern the oil sands raise in Canadians.
  Three hundred years ago, a European explorer discovering the natural and vast country that would become Canada called bitumen worthless. Today, no one would make such a mistake.

What are the Oil sands?

A resource like no other

Origin of the Oil Sands

Canada is known for its plethora of natural resources - vast forests of green, plentiful waterways tangling the landscape, rolling plains of golden wheat. Metres under the surface of the oil sands, often underneath tons of soil, rock and plantlife, lies bitumen - a resource unlike the rest.


   “I think for the longest time we have moved from one natural resource to another,” said Stephen Scott, Carleton University’s graduate supervisor of sustainable energy policy. “If you look at the history of Canada, it’s kind of a staples approach; going from cart to beaver pelts to lumber then oil, natural gas. Just taking stuff out of the ground - lots of mining and selling it raw to the world.”

Abundance

Oil is found around the globe, but the oil sands are more unique in nature. The world’s largest oil sands are found in Canada and Venezuela, with smaller pockets found in the United States, Russia, Madagascar and the Republic of Congo.

    Bitumen is a heavy oil. Difficult to extract from both the ground it is in and the sand and clay the oil is mixed with, bitumen presented a challenge to those wanting to extract the valuable oil it contained. Unlike bitumen’s cousin, crude oil, which flows freely and is found in a purer form, bitumen requires purification and warming before resembling the oil within.

Where it's found

  Oil sands were created millennia ago, when shifting tectonic plates formed the Rocky Mountains and in turn buried the sedimentary rock layers under Alberta. The result was a pressure cooker of heat that transformed shale rock into natural gas and light oil. Because of plate shifting and pinching underground, a tilted tectonic plate allowed light oil to migrate north. There, where the future oil sands would form, microbial biodegradation thicked and immobilized the oil in Northern Alberta.

   Bitumen remained untouched in the oil sands for centuries, tucked deep within the earth, protected by shale and rock and soil. Small dribbles would escape along waterways, where Indigenous Peoples would use the tar-like substance to waterproof their canoes. Early European explorers discounted bitumen, but later pioneers and government researchers realized the commercial possibilities contained in the oil sands.

Bitumen Extraction

  The process of extracting oil from the oil sands is complex because of the resources’ impurities and surface depth.
  Bitumen closer to the surface can be extracted by way of open pit mining. Once bitumen is discovered, the land is cleared and bitumen is shoveled up. The bitumen is then crushed and pumped with hot water for transport to the settling basin, or tailing pond. There, the wet mixture is allowed to settle: bitumen froth rises to the separation vessel’s surface and the sediments - rock and clay - sink to the bottom. The bitumen can then be collected for future upgrading and refining. While the bitumen is extracted, the remnants of the extraction process remain in tailing ponds: most ponds are re-used, but the landscape is indefinitely changed throughout the process. A significant portion of the water can be recycled, but requires treatment to cleanse it.

Bitumen Extraction cont.

  The majority of the oil sands — around 80 per cent — are too deep for surface mining. Bitumen with a surface depth of 130 metres or more is extracted using in-situ techniques, which disturb the earth’s surface to a lesser degree than open pit mining. Steam-assisted gravity drainage (SAGD) is the most used method of in-situ mining, recovering 55 to 60 per cent of extractable bitumen. Two parallel wells are drilled, one higher than the other. The higher injection well continually inserts steam into the ground, heating the bitumen and allowing it to become more fluid.
  The liquid bitumen flows lower and is brought into the lower well, collected with pumps and transported for upgrading.
